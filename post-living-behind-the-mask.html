Living behind the mask

I learned to “act normal” long before i understood why everything felt difficult. In school, with my family, even in relationships — everyone expected me to be more social, more talkative, more spontaneous. But i did not know how.

When people made jokes, i laughed even if I did not understand them. I tried to talk more even when my words felt stuck, and i copied the way others behaved so I could blend in.

On the outside, it looked like i was fine. But inside, i was working — constantly.

“Masking is not pretending to be someone else. It is trying to be the version of you that people wont reject.”

And there was something else too. I stutter. So on top of trying to understand people, i was also trying to control my voice. The more nervous i felt, the harder it became.

Sometimes, because of all that effort, i came across as distant or cold. People thought i did not care, or that i lacked empathy. But the truth was the opposite: I was feeling everything too deeply — i just did not know how to show it in the way others expected.

I also took things literally. I understood words exactly as they were said, and not always the hidden meanings, sarcasm, or “between the lines” messages. That led to misunderstandings — not because i did not care, but because i was speaking honestly, directly, and in my own language.

After social situations, i often felt ashamed — like i had done everything wrong, even when no one had actually said anything.

That is the part of autism people does not see: the silent effort, the invisible work, the exhaustion that comes from simply trying to exist in a world that does not speak your language.
